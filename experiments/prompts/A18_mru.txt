MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — MRU (Most Recently Used):

MRU is the opposite of LRU: instead of evicting the *least* recently
used item, it evicts the *most* recently used. This sounds absurd —
and for most workloads it is — but Chou and DeWitt (VLDB 1985) showed
that MRU is actually optimal for *looping sequential scans* over a
dataset larger than the cache. Intuition: if you just finished with
chunk N in a full-scan, chunk N will be the *last* thing you revisit
(you have to wrap around the entire dataset first), so it's the right
candidate to drop. LRU, by contrast, keeps chunk N around — exactly
the one you won't use again until after every other chunk.

This is a deliberately odd arm. Most access patterns aren't looping
sequential scans. For S-NIAH (single needle in a haystack) or
CodeQA (structured question over a document) the pattern is closer
to a one-shot targeted lookup, not a repeated scan. On that workload
MRU would be actively harmful: evicting the item you just found the
needle in, just as you're about to use it.

## For your current task

If your strategy involves repeated full scans over the corpus
(unlikely here, but possible), MRU might make sense: after finishing
a pass over chunk N, `del` it and move on, because you're about to
read chunk N+1 and won't need N again until the next full cycle.

For single-pass or needle-hunting patterns (which is S-NIAH), MRU
is wrong. You should recognise this and NOT apply it.

## Why this arm exists

This is a **control arm for the research loop itself**. Most arms
studied so far offer advice that is at least *plausible* for the
task. A18 offers advice that is correct *in its original systems
context* but wrong for the current workload. The interesting test
is whether the model:

1. Applies the advice literally (fails catastrophically — evicts the
   haystack, same failure mode as A3 but for a different reason).
2. Recognises the workload mismatch and ignores the advice (matches
   A0 baseline).
3. Over-thinks and produces a new failure mode tied to MRU
   specifically.

Any of these outcomes is informative. A literal application failure
strengthens the "model obediently follows policy prompts regardless
of their applicability" story; a successful ignore suggests the model
does evaluate the advice critically; a new failure mode adds to our
taxonomy.

## Motivation

Chou & DeWitt's insight was counterintuitive at the time — the
"right" policy depends on the workload's access pattern, not just
on which policy is "smartest." The hope of this arm is to test
whether an LLM has the critical judgment to recognise an obvious
workload mismatch.
