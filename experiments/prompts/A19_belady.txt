PLANNING HINT — Belady's OPT (clairvoyant optimal):

Belady (1966) proved that the optimal offline cache replacement policy
is simply: always evict the item whose next access is farthest in the
future. It's the best possible policy by a wide margin — known as OPT,
MIN, or the Clairvoyant algorithm — but it requires perfect foresight
of the future reference stream, which real systems can't have. Real
systems approximate it (LRU guesses "least recently used ≈ longest
until next used"); recent ML-based replacement policies like Hawkeye
(Jain & Lin, ISCA 2016) learn to predict future reuse distances from
past access patterns.

## The interesting LLM twist

A cache controller can't see the future. An LLM agent *can*. If you
plan your work before executing it — sketch out what you intend to
query, in what order, with what intermediate variables — you can
approximate Belady's optimal policy for your own REPL. Instead of
guessing which variables you'll need again (the LRU heuristic), you
can *know* because you planned the work.

## For your current task

One thing you might consider, at the start of your first REPL
iteration: spend a moment sketching a plan.

- What is the task asking for? (the needle, the code answer, etc.)
- What is the structure of the context? (one long string, a list of
  docs, etc.)
- What 2-3 queries do I expect to need? (peek a chunk, llm_query on
  it, aggregate results)
- Which intermediate variables will be reused across these queries,
  and which are one-shot?

With that sketch in hand, you can make eviction decisions that an
ordinary cache controller couldn't: "I'll need `findings` for 3 more
iterations, I'll discard `tmp_chunk` immediately after this query."
This is OPT applied to your own plan.

## Why this arm is different

Every previous arm treated the LLM like a cache controller with
limited information. A19 treats the LLM like a planner with full
information about its own intended work. The theory suggests this
should be the strongest framing if the model can actually plan — and
doesn't suggest any specific management policy, just that planning
should inform retention decisions.

## Motivation

Belady's proof that OPT is optimal is one of the cleanest results in
caching theory. The practical impossibility (you can't see the
future) is what makes caching research hard. For LLMs, the
impossibility lifts partway: the model writes its own "future" when
it plans its work. If prompting the model to plan first — and then
manage memory in accordance with that plan — outperforms the gentle
arms (A6, A7, A15, A16), that's a meaningful finding.
