MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — LIRS (reuse distance):

LIRS (Jiang & Zhang, SIGMETRICS 2002) uses a different locality metric
than LRU. Instead of tracking "how recently was this accessed," LIRS
tracks each item's *Inter-Reference Recency* (IRR): the number of
distinct other items accessed between the last two references to this
one. A small IRR means the item is genuinely reused tightly; a large
IRR means other things keep coming in between, so the item's true
reuse distance is long.

The cache splits into two partitions:

- **LIR (Low Inter-Reference Recency)** — items with small IRR. These
  get priority residency; LIRS keeps them all memory-resident.
- **HIR (High Inter-Reference Recency)** — items with large or
  unknown IRR. Most of these live as metadata only (not resident);
  a small fraction are resident as "trial" candidates that can be
  promoted to LIR on an IRR improvement.

LIRS outperforms LRU especially for workloads with weak locality —
scans, sequential access, loops — where LRU keeps the wrong items.

## For your current task

The LIRS mental model for your REPL: for each variable you keep,
estimate not just "when did I last touch this" but "how many *other*
things have I worked with in between." If a variable was referenced
at iteration 3 and iteration 5 (one other thing in between), its IRR
is 1 — tight reuse, worth keeping. If referenced at iteration 3 and
iteration 8 (four things in between), IRR is 4 — less tight reuse,
lower priority for retention.

One concrete suggestion: when you notice yourself considering whether
to `del` a variable, ask "since I last touched this, how many OTHER
things have I used?" A small answer = it's in your LIR set. A large
answer = it's in your HIR set and probably not worth keeping.

## Motivation

LIRS represents a meaningful theoretical advance — it introduced the
reuse-distance framework that later influenced ARC, ClockPro, and many
modern policies. Testing whether the mental model translates to an
LLM context is an interesting falsification opportunity for the
refined hypothesis.
