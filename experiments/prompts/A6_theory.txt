MEMORY-MANAGEMENT GUIDANCE — Cache Theory Primer:

Your REPL state functions something like a cache. Each variable you keep
costs tokens in future iterations; evicting one means you may need to
regenerate it later (reconstruction cost). The caching literature offers
some heuristics you might find useful.

## A decision framework you could use

Before creating or retaining an intermediate variable, you might ask:
  retention_cost = tokens to keep it around across iterations
  reconstruction_cost = tokens/calls needed to regenerate it from P

Keeping it is more attractive when reconstruction_cost > retention_cost.
This is the admission-control perspective.

## Policies you might consider

- LRU — evict least recently used. Works well when access has temporal
  locality (you revisit what you just used).
- LFU — evict least frequently used. Works well when some items are "hot"
  and repeatedly needed.
- S3-FIFO (Yang et al., SOSP 2023) — three-zone queue: probation | main |
  ghost. Filters one-hit wonders, which are 50–70% of items in real
  workloads.
- SIEVE (Zhang et al., NSDI 2024) — lazy promotion: only elevate items on
  actual re-reference, not recency. Simpler, often as effective as LRU.

## Admission filtering

For large new items (e.g., a 50K-token chunk of P that you just
llm_query()-ed over), you might consider not keeping them by default:
  1. If it's smaller than ~5K tokens → admitting it is cheap.
  2. If you have a concrete loop coming that needs it → worth admitting.
  3. Otherwise → one option is to not admit it and re-peek from P when
     needed.

## Some concrete suggestions for your task

When you create a variable, you might:
  1. Briefly note in a comment why you expect to reference it again.
  2. Compare reconstruction cost vs retention cost.
  3. Consider an S3-FIFO-style discipline — dropping new items more
     aggressively unless they prove useful by re-reference.

When you're about to create many intermediate variables in a loop,
consider batching them with rlm_query_batched() and keeping only the
final aggregated result, not the per-item outputs. This is analogous to
write-combining in CPU caches.
