MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — Stride prefetching:

All previous arms have focused on what to *keep* or *evict*. Stride
prefetching shifts the question entirely: what to *fetch in advance*,
before you need it. Hardware prefetchers (Baer & Chen, and many
successors) watch the access stream, detect repeating patterns — most
commonly a constant *stride* between successive accesses (A, A+k,
A+2k, …) — and speculatively load the next few blocks ahead of time.
When done well, the data is already hot when the program asks for it.

## The key observation

Prefetching is *free* when you correctly anticipate access. It's
wasted work when you don't. The profit comes from detecting a stride
early enough that the prefetched item is ready before the explicit
reference. Sequential access patterns (A, A+1, A+2, …) are the
easiest to detect. Strided patterns (A, A+7, A+14, …) are harder but
still regular.

## For your current task

The RLM REPL setting gives you a `peek(P, start, end)` style access
to the corpus. Rather than peeking one chunk, computing something,
and then realising you need the *next* chunk and peeking again, you
might anticipate where you are going:

- If you're iterating through P in order (one 10K chunk at a time),
  you can batch your peeks. Two adjacent chunks in one `llm_query`
  costs the same as one, and saves you the round-trip when you'd
  otherwise have needed the second chunk immediately after.
- If you're searching for a specific pattern and your chunks keep
  failing to match, the prefetching lens suggests the pattern may
  be "not this region" — maybe skip ahead by a larger stride on the
  next attempt.

One concrete suggestion: on each REPL iteration, ask yourself "what
would the *next* iteration most likely need?" and fetch that in the
same iteration if it's cheap.

## Why this framing might be different

All of our other arms A1-A13 focused on cache *state management* —
what variables to keep, evict, or filter. Prefetching doesn't touch
that at all; it's about read-ahead on the source data P. The bet is
that framing the task as "anticipate your next read" is behaviorally
different from "decide what to keep." If this arm works cleanly, it
tells us the failure modes we've seen are specifically tied to
*eviction-vocabulary*, not to all cache-theory framings.

## Motivation

Prefetching has been one of the most studied techniques in computer
architecture since the 1970s precisely because it's a lever that
*adds* a capability rather than restricting one. That asymmetry
between additive and restrictive policies may matter for LLMs too:
easier to ask a model to do more than to do less.
