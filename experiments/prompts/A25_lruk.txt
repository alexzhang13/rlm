MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — LRU-K (O'Neil, O'Neil & Weikum 1993):

Ordinary LRU (A1) makes an eviction decision based on the *single*
last-access time per item. LRU-K generalizes this by tracking the
*K most recent* access times per item, then evicting the one whose
backward-K-distance (the age of its Kth most recent access) is
largest. Intuition: a single recent touch can be noise — a scan, a
one-shot reference. Seeing K recent touches is strong evidence of
ongoing relevance. For K=2, LRU-2 dramatically outperforms LRU on
database buffer workloads because it filters scan-pollution without
needing the frequency-counting bookkeeping of LFU.

The paper also proved an optimality result: LRU-K is optimal among
policies that use only the last-K access timestamps.

## For your current task

Consider tracking, per intermediate variable, *two* mental timestamps
rather than one: "when did I last touch it" and "when did I touch
it before that." A variable touched just once (its second-last
access is undefined) is a weaker keep candidate than one touched
twice in close succession.

Concretely, with K=2:
- A variable you created and never re-referenced: weakest. Evict
  first under pressure.
- A variable you referenced twice recently: strongest. Keep even
  under pressure.
- A variable you created, then referenced once, then forgot for many
  iterations: medium. Maybe evict if quota is tight.

## Where this lands

This is A1 LRU (recency) + numerical K-history bookkeeping. The
refined hypothesis predicts it should be at least as bad as A1
(0.67) and probably worse — more metadata to track.

## Motivation

LRU-K became a standard in database buffer pool management after its
SIGMOD93 publication. It's the classical mid-point between LRU and
LFU, and its optimality proof makes it a theoretically clean test
case — any failure to translate to the LLM setting is interesting
precisely because the algorithm is provably good under natural
assumptions.
