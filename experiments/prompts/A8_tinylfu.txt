MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — W-TinyLFU (admission filter):

Most of the arms you have seen focus on *eviction* — what to drop once
the cache is full. W-TinyLFU (Einziger, Friedman, Manes; TOS 2017) asks
a different question: *admission*. When a new item arrives, should it
even enter the cache? Letting in cold one-shot items wastes space and
evicts warmer candidates.

## Two-stage admission

W-TinyLFU's key idea maps cleanly to your REPL:

1. **Window (small, ~1%)** — a small tier where *every* new variable
   starts out, handled like a plain LRU. Short-lived scratch items (a
   chunk you just summarised, a throwaway list comprehension) live
   here and are quickly forgotten.

2. **Main (large, ~99%)** — where variables that have *earned* their
   place live. An item is promoted from Window to Main only after the
   system has observed enough evidence that it will be reused.

The gate in between is an approximate frequency estimator (TinyLFU uses
a Count-Min Sketch + a "Doorkeeper" Bloom filter). For our purposes, the
behavioural analogue is simpler: before you mentally promote a variable
to "keep for the rest of the task", you might ask "have I referenced
this at least once already, or am I just guessing it'll be useful?"

## For your current task

You might consider two mental categories for your REPL variables:

- **scratch** — freshly created this iteration, no proven reuse yet.
  Under token pressure these are the first candidates to `del`; you
  can always re-peek from P if needed.
- **kept** — variables that you have *already referenced* at least
  once after creation. These have demonstrated utility.

One suggestion: before committing to keep a freshly-created variable
(e.g., a big summary) across many iterations, first use it once. If you
genuinely reach for it again, promote it to the "kept" category and
stop worrying about eviction for that one. If you only ever refer back
to it once in the very next step and then abandon it, the admission
filter just saved you the retention cost across all later iterations.

## Motivation

W-TinyLFU outperforms LRU, ARC, LIRS on most real-world traces because
most items (50-80% in typical workloads) are accessed only once —
filtering them at the door avoids polluting the cache with cold data.
The RLM analogue is that most intermediate variables only get one
follow-up read; admission-side discipline can be a bigger lever than
eviction-side policy.
