MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — Two-Queue (2Q) (Johnson & Shasha, VLDB 1994):

2Q, like S3-FIFO and ARC, separates variables into tiers based on how
much evidence of reuse they've accumulated. It was first articulated in
1994 as a simpler alternative to LRU-K; the key structural idea is two
independent queues:

1. **A1 (secondary buffer)** — where every newly-referenced variable
   lands on first access. It's a fast-eviction FIFO: items that are
   never re-referenced drift to the exit end and get discarded. A1 is
   meant to be the filter — cold one-shot items never enter the warm
   cache.

2. **Am (main buffer, LRU-managed)** — once a variable in A1 gets
   referenced a second time, it's promoted here. Am holds "proven hot"
   variables and is managed as an ordinary LRU.

The paper's insight was simple: don't let a single access earn cache
residency. Require two. Johnson and Shasha showed 2Q approached the
hit-rate of LRU-K while being much cheaper to implement.

## Different from S3-FIFO (A3)

2Q has no "ghost" zone — it never tracks evicted items' histories —
and no reinsertion on A1-miss. It's strictly: A1 is a pre-filter, Am is
the cache, promotion happens exactly once per item (on the first
re-reference).

## For your current task

Consider this mental scheme for REPL variables:

- **A1** — newly-created variables. Expect these to be one-hit wonders
  unless evidence suggests otherwise. Under token pressure, look here
  first to `del`.
- **Am** — variables you've referenced for a second time. These have
  earned a spot; treat them like LRU (evict the least-recently-touched
  only when Am itself fills up).

One suggestion you might try: after you create an intermediate variable
(e.g., a summary string, a chunk list), note it mentally as "A1". The
very next time you find yourself typing its name in a `repl` block,
silently promote it to "Am". You don't need to do any bookkeeping
beyond this single toggle.

## Why this framing might work here

The critical difference from A3/A4's directive phrasing is that 2Q
makes the promotion event *passive* — it's just "the second time you
use it" — rather than prescribing an active sweep or pre-emptive
discard. The model doesn't have to decide when to run the policy; the
policy triggers itself on natural reference events.
