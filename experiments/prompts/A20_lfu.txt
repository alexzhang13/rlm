MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — LFU with aging (LFUDA):

LFU (Least Frequently Used) takes a different angle from recency-based
policies: it tracks an access-count *per item* and evicts the one
with the smallest count. The classical critique is "cache pollution":
an item that was hot for a brief burst accumulates a high count and
sticks around long after it stops being useful. LFUDA (LFU with
Dynamic Aging) fixes this by adding a cache-age offset when an item
is created or re-referenced, so old counts decay relative to new
ones.

## Translating to your REPL

The analog is a mental access-count per intermediate variable. You
might maintain something like: "I've referenced `findings` three
times, `chunk_12` once." When token pressure forces an eviction
choice, prefer dropping the low-count one. Add an aging twist to
avoid stuck-popular entries — subtract 1 from every count at natural
checkpoints (end of a scan, completion of a sub-task).

## For your current task

This is deliberately similar in tone to A1 (LRU) — recency-based —
but the count is frequency-based. The test is whether frequency
vocabulary breaks the model differently than recency vocabulary.

Concretely, you might try: at the start of each REPL iteration,
briefly ask yourself "which of my current variables have I used more
than once?" Keep those. Discard single-use variables that you don't
anticipate needing.

## Motivation

LFU is the other classical replacement policy alongside LRU — both
taught as canonical cache systems in undergrad architecture courses.
Together they cover "recency" and "frequency" as the two elementary
access-pattern signals. Testing both in the LLM setting gives us a
controlled comparison of these two axes.
