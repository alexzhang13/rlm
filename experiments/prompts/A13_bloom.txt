MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — Bloom-filter admission:

A Bloom filter (Bloom, 1970) is a space-efficient probabilistic data
structure for answering membership queries. It never says "definitely
in set" — only "possibly in set" or "definitely not in set." Its cache
application: to prevent *one-hit wonders* from wasting cache space, use
a Bloom filter to remember which items have been accessed at least
once. On the first access, the item is recorded in the filter but not
admitted to the cache; only on the second access (when the filter
already contains the item) does the item earn cache residency.

The key property is space: ~10 bits per element for a 1% false-positive
rate, regardless of element size. For a real cache that's trivial
overhead. For an LLM mentally tracking membership, the analog is
maintaining a much shorter *set* of identifiers — not the items
themselves, just their names or fingerprints.

## For your current task

One thing you might try: before committing to retain a freshly-derived
intermediate variable across iterations, ask yourself "have I seen
something conceptually-similar before in this task?" If yes, keep it.
If no, treat it as a tentative scratch item — reference it, use it,
but don't invest in retaining it.

In practice you might keep a rolling ~5-item mental list of short
descriptors for the things you've done so far (e.g. "chunk-summary",
"query-tokens", "haystack-offset"). When you derive something new,
check that list. Hit = likely worth keeping. Miss = likely a one-hit
wonder; let it die at iteration's end.

## Why this shape is deliberately lightweight

Unlike A11 (SLRU: probationary/protected/demoted) or A12 (victim
cache: 3-4 memo slots of content), Bloom-filter admission needs almost
no state — a list of short identifiers, not full content snapshots.
The intention is to test whether *extremely low-overhead* admission
framing pushes behavior more cleanly than heavier machinery. If A13
matches A0 baseline while A11/A12 diverge, that's evidence that the
cognitive weight of the policy matters as much as the policy itself.

## Motivation

Bloom filters became one of the most widely used probabilistic data
structures in systems precisely because they trade a little accuracy
(false positives) for dramatic savings in space and time. The bet
here is that similar accuracy/overhead tradeoffs may apply when an
LLM is doing bookkeeping in prose: a rough "have I seen this kind
of thing?" check might be easier to maintain than a rigorous count
or ordered list, and still add useful admission discipline.
