ALGORITHM-DESIGN HINT — Cache-oblivious recursion:

This hint is deliberately NOT a cache-management policy. Rather, it
suggests an algorithmic shape: decompose your task by recursive
divide-and-conquer, and let locality emerge from the structure of the
recursion itself. Frigo, Leiserson, Prokop & Ramachandran (1999)
showed that recursive algorithms with the right shape — halving the
problem at each step — achieve asymptotically optimal cache behavior
*without any tuning parameter* for the cache size. The base case fits
in cache; each recursive level processes a subproblem small enough
that its children also fit; locality is automatic.

## The key idea

Instead of thinking "which items should I keep in cache?", think about
the *structure of the work*. If you arrange the computation so that
subproblems halve in size, then by the time you're working on any
subproblem, everything it touches already fits in whatever-size
cache you have. The cache parameter never appears in the algorithm,
yet performance is near-optimal.

Classic examples:
- Matrix transpose: recursively split the matrix into halves; at
  the leaf, do the transpose element-by-element.
- FFT (van Loan): decompose the DFT recursively rather than using
  an ordered iterative loop.
- Sorting: funnel-sort / merge-sort structured around divide-and-conquer.

## For your current task

The RLM REPL setting hands you a large `context` variable. The
cache-oblivious lens suggests asking:

- Can the task be structured recursively? "Is the answer in the first
  half?" vs "Is the answer in the second half?" — and if it's in a
  half, recurse on that half only; if spread across, merge results.
- Each recursive step should halve the work. If you're looking for a
  single needle in a large haystack, binary-split-and-query is a
  natural fit: one `llm_query` for "is the pattern in this half?",
  then recurse.

Notice this framing never says anything about managing or evicting
REPL variables. It only talks about access structure of the work.

## Why this shape might matter

All of the recent arms (A11-A14) tried to talk about *state* in
various ways — tiers, memos, admission, prefetch queues — and many of
them caused the model to spend tokens on meta-discussion of that
state. Cache-oblivious framing doesn't touch state at all; it tells
the model how to structure its queries. If A15 performs well on
S-NIAH (where recursive halving is a natural strategy), that supports
the hypothesis that eviction-vocabulary, not cache-vocabulary, is the
culprit.

## Motivation

Frigo et al.'s original paper was among the most cited in theory of
caching. The asymmetric beauty of cache-oblivious algorithms is that
they ignore the cache entirely and still optimize for it. The question
here is whether a comparably detached framing lets an LLM perform
well without the overhead of modeling its own memory.
