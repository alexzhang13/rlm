CACHE-PARTITIONING HINT — UCP / way allocation (Qureshi, MICRO 2006):

Shared last-level caches in multi-core systems have a fairness
problem: the LRU policy implicitly gives more space to whichever
process is most demanding, not whichever most benefits. Qureshi's
Utility-Based Cache Partitioning (UCP, MICRO 2006) proposed explicitly
*dividing* the cache ways among processes based on how much each
process's miss rate drops per way given to it. Low-benefit
processes get fewer ways; high-benefit processes get more. The
technique generalizes to per-task partitioning within a single
process too.

## The key insight

Partitioning is a structural decision: *before* workload runs, decide
how much cache each logical stream gets. Then each stream manages its
partition however it wants (usually LRU). This separates the
allocation question ("how much space?") from the replacement question
("what to evict?"). Partitioning prevents one heavy workload from
evicting everything a light workload needs.

## For your current task

In RLM terms: at the start of your work, divide your mental "variable
budget" among the logical sub-tasks you expect to perform:

- one budget allocation for *holding the prompt / context* (needs to
  persist)
- one for *iteration-local scratch* (short-lived, can churn)
- one for *running findings* (grows over time, needs retention)

Don't let scratch variables crowd out findings just because you
created scratch more recently; the two are in different partitions.
Under token pressure, drop from the scratch partition first; touch
findings and context only as a last resort.

## Motivation

Cache partitioning is one of the cleanest examples of structural
rather than dynamic cache optimization — the policy does nothing
during the workload, only before. If LLMs handle structural prompts
better than stateful ones (as A15, A16, A19 suggest), partitioning
should land cleanly on the PASS side.
