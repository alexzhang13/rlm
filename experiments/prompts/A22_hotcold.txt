MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — Hot/Cold tiering (CacheLib):

Facebook's CacheLib and similar production caches distinguish between
*hot* items (frequently or recently accessed, kept in fast DRAM) and
*cold* items (rarely accessed, kept in larger but slower SSD or remote
storage). This is a binary tier distinction — no gradients of "how
hot" or "how cold," just a single bit per item. Cold items that start
getting accessed can be promoted; hot items that go quiet get demoted.

The binary simplicity is the point: tracking "hot or cold" is
cognitively lighter than tracking "how many times, how recently, what
IRR." Production systems found this crude tier distinction delivers
most of the benefit of more elaborate schemes.

## For your current task

One thing you might try is labelling each intermediate variable with
a single mental bit:

- **HOT** — variables you expect to use repeatedly in the next few
  iterations (your current `findings` dict, the main chunk list).
- **COLD** — variables you created but don't plan to revisit soon
  (a temporary summary you already used, a chunk you've moved past).

Under token pressure, drop cold items first. Keep hot items. Don't
maintain a ranking beyond this one-bit split.

A natural promotion/demotion: when you `peek` a chunk that's been
sitting cold, it becomes hot. When you finish a phase where you kept
something hot and move on to a new phase, mark it cold and consider
evicting.

## Where this lands in the taxonomy

Binary categorical state is simpler than LFU's counters (A20) or
LIRS's IRR values (A21). If the CRASH hypothesis is correct — that
*numerical bookkeeping* crashes the model while *binary categorical
bookkeeping* runs — A22 should produce valid cells. If it also
crashes or fails, the CRASH bucket correlates with something else
(not numerical complexity).

## Motivation

CacheLib powers 70+ production systems at Facebook precisely because
its hot/cold abstraction is simple enough to reason about while still
capturing the dominant signal. For an LLM doing its own cache
accounting, the same argument applies: one bit of metadata per
variable is tractable, while an ordered list or integer counter is
not.
