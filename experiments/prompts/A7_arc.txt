MEMORY-MANAGEMENT HINT — ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache):

ARC (Megiddo & Modha, FAST 2003) proposes that *both* recency and
frequency matter, and the optimal balance between them shifts with the
workload. Rather than committing to one signal, you might maintain a
mental split of your REPL variables into two groups:

  RECENCY pool  — variables you've created or touched once, and haven't
                 yet referenced a second time.
  FREQUENCY pool — variables you've referenced *more than once*. The
                 second reference "promotes" the variable out of the
                 recency pool.

When deciding what to `del` under token pressure, one option is to evict
the least-recently-used item from the *RECENCY* pool first — these are
one-hit candidates whose reuse value hasn't been demonstrated yet. Only
when the recency pool is exhausted should you reach into the frequency
pool.

## The adaptive twist

ARC's real innovation is the *ghost list*: when you delete a variable,
keep a small comment noting what it held and where it came from (which
chunk of P, which sub-call). If later in the task you find yourself
reconstructing a variable that matches a ghost entry, that's a signal
the recency/frequency balance is off for this workload — you should
become slightly MORE generous about retaining similar new variables
going forward. If ghost hits are rare, your current balance is fine.

## For your current task

Consider tagging each intermediate variable mentally with a "seen-count"
as you reference it. On eviction, prefer items with count=1 over count≥2.
After you `del` a variable, briefly note its identity in a comment. If
the same reconstruction happens twice, bias toward retention next time.

## Motivation

ARC is a self-tuning policy: instead of assuming "recency wins" (LRU) or
"frequency wins" (LFU), it adapts based on observed evictions that turned
out to be premature. In your REPL setting, you don't get formal ghost
lists, but the reflection loop — "did I just regenerate something I
threw away?" — is the same signal. If you notice yourself doing that,
retain more aggressively for the rest of the task.
